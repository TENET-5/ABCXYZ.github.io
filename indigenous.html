Section 1 — Residential Schools
Indian Residential Schools — 139 Schools, 150,000+ Children, Confirmed Deaths, Unmarked Graves
CULTURAL GENOCIDE Federal + Churches 1870s–1996 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

The Indian Residential School system operated 139 federally funded schools, managed primarily by the Roman Catholic, Anglican, United, and Presbyterian churches. Beginning in the 1870s and running until the last school closed in 1996 (Gordon Residential School, Saskatchewan), an estimated 150,000 First Nations, Métis, and Inuit children were forcibly removed from their families. Attendance was compulsory under the Indian Act; RCMP officers were used to remove children from communities.

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2015) documented at least 4,100 confirmed child deaths in the schools. The National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation (NCTR) now estimates over 6,000 deaths, based on ongoing archival research. Actual deaths may number between 6,000 and 25,000 according to historical researchers who note that death records were incomplete, falsified, or never kept. Causes included tuberculosis, influenza, physical abuse, malnutrition, and deliberate medical experimentation. Many children were buried in unmarked graves on school grounds without family notification.

The TRC's 2015 Final Report used the term "cultural genocide." The Harper government declined to formally accept the "genocide" designation, offering a formal apology in 2008 (PM Harper) for the system's existence without using that word. The Trudeau government accepted the TRC report and pledged to implement all 94 Calls to Action; as of 2024, 13 are complete.

"The Canadian government pursued this policy of cultural genocide because it wished to divest itself of its legal and financial obligations to Aboriginal people and, as it believed, to gain control over their land and resources. If anything, the history of the residential schools demonstrates that a government can establish a policy aimed at destroying the cultural identity of a people." — TRC Final Report, Volume 1, "A History," p. 3, December 2015

Section 2 — TRC 94 Calls to Action: Implementation Status
Truth and Reconciliation Commission — 94 Calls to Action: 13 Complete After 9 Years
SYSTEMIC FAILURE Federal + Provincial 2015–2024 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada, chaired by Justice Murray Sinclair, released its Final Report and 94 Calls to Action in December 2015. The Commission documented the experiences of survivors of the Indian Residential School System and found the system constituted "cultural genocide." The Calls to Action addressed child welfare, education, language and culture, health, justice, and reconciliation — spanning federal, provincial, and territorial jurisdictions as well as the private sector and churches.

The Yellowhead Institute tracks implementation annually. As of 2023–2024: 13 calls fully completed, 44 in progress, 37 not started or stalled. Nine years after publication, the majority of calls remain unimplemented.

The National Inquiry into MMIWG, mandated September 2016 and reporting June 3, 2019, heard testimony from more than 2,380 family members, survivors, experts and knowledge keepers over two and a half years. The Final Report — Reclaiming Power and Place — concluded that the violence against Indigenous women and girls constitutes a "race-based genocide" under international law (UNDRIP; the Genocide Convention), carried out through colonial policies and ongoing state and institutional failures including police failures to investigate, protect, and inform families.

The inquiry found the RCMP's own 2014 report (1,181 cases, 1980–2012) was an undercount — acknowledging cases were undercounted due to poor data collection, failure to record Indigenous identity, and inadequate investigation. The Native Women's Association of Canada Database tracks over 16,000 cases. The inquiry found systemic RCMP failures including inadequate investigation, dismissal of cases, harassment of families, and racism. The RCMP's National Centre for Missing Persons and Unidentified Remains lacked resources throughout the inquiry period.

"The violence the National Inquiry heard about amounts to a race-based genocide of Indigenous Peoples, including First Nations, Métis and Inuit, carried out by the Canadian state." — MMIWG Final Report, "A Legal Analysis of Genocide," p. 50, June 2019
"Canada has failed to take adequate and appropriate measures to prevent and address violence against Indigenous women and girls, and as a result their security and equality rights as recognized in the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms are not protected." — MMIWG Final Report, Vol. 1a, Summary, p. 8

Government Response (2021–2024): PM Trudeau accepted the Final Report June 3, 2019, and pledged a National Action Plan. The National Action Plan was released June 3, 2021 — two years after the report, one day before advocates planned protests over delays. Advocates, commissioners, and First Nations organizations stated the plan is insufficiently funded, lacks binding timelines, and does not commit to measurable targets. As of 2024, no comprehensive RCMP oversight reform has been implemented. The RCMP's operational independence from political accountability on MMIWG cases remains unaddressed.

Sources: MMIWG Final Report "Reclaiming Power and Place," June 3, 2019; RCMP Missing and Murdered Aboriginal Women Report 2014; NWAC database; National Action Plan in Response to the MMIWG Final Report, June 2021; Amnesty International Canada MMIWG accountability tracking 2022–2024.

Section 4 — Child Welfare: Jordan's Principle, CHRT, and Sixties Scoop
Jordan's Principle & CHRT — Canada Found in Contempt; $40B Compensation Ordered
DISCRIMINATION Federal Liberal 2007–2024 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Jordan's Principle — named for Jordan River Anderson, a First Nations child from Norway House Cree Nation who died in hospital in 2005 while the federal and provincial governments disputed who would pay for his home care — requires the government of first contact to pay for services First Nations children need without delay, resolving jurisdictional disputes afterwards. The Canadian Human Rights Tribunal (CHRT) first found Canada discriminatory in its underfunding of on-reserve child and family services in January 2016.

Canada appealed and lost repeatedly. In 2019 the CHRT found Canada in contempt for failing to comply with the 2016 order — a finding that the government spent more money fighting the case in court than it would have cost to simply provide the services children needed. A 2011 Auditor General report had already found ISC underfunded First Nations child welfare by $321M. In 2022, after years of litigation, Canada negotiated a settlement: $20B in compensation to First Nations children and families harmed by the discriminatory child welfare system since 1991, plus $20B for long-term reform — totalling $40B. As of 2024, individual payment processing is ongoing with advocates reporting delays.

Statistics: First Nations children represent 52% of all children in foster care in Canada, while comprising approximately 4% of the total child population. This over-representation is directly linked to underfunded services, poverty caused by underfunded community programs, and cultural bias in the welfare system itself.

"Canada has been wilfully and recklessly discriminating against 165,000 First Nations children for years. The government spent more money fighting Indigenous children in court than it would have cost to simply provide them the services they needed." — Cindy Blackstock, First Nations Child & Family Caring Society, parliamentary testimony 2019

Sources: CHRT Decision 2016 CHRT 2 (First Nations Child and Family Caring Society v. Attorney General of Canada); CHRT Contempt Finding 2019 CHRT 39; $40B settlement agreement in principle August 2022; Federal Court approval process 2023; AG report on child welfare underfunding 2011; Statistics Canada child welfare data 2022.

Sixties Scoop — 20,000–40,000 Children Removed; $750M Federal Settlement (2018)
SYSTEMIC FAILURE Federal 1950s–1980s | Settlement 2018 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Between the 1950s and early 1980s, the federal and provincial governments facilitated the mass removal of Indigenous children from their families and communities and placed them with non-Indigenous families — largely without consent, without notification to band councils, and in violation of any recognition of First Nations jurisdiction over child welfare. An estimated 20,000–40,000 Indigenous children were removed; many were adopted out to families in the United States, Australia, and Europe. Children lost their languages, cultures, names, and connections to family and community. Many reported physical and sexual abuse in adoptive homes.

The name "Sixties Scoop" was coined by researcher Patrick Johnston in his 1983 report "Native Children and the Child Welfare System" for the Canadian Council on Social Development. In 2017, Ontario Superior Court Justice Edward Belobaba certified a class action — finding the federal Crown owed a duty of care to ensure Indigenous identity was preserved and breached it. In 2018, the federal government agreed to a $750M settlement covering approximately 22,000 eligible First Nations claimants. Métis and Inuit children were not initially covered; subsequent provincial and federal settlements addressed some of these groups. Many survivors reported the settlement process was itself traumatic and insufficient.

Sources: Brown v. Canada (Attorney General), 2017 ONSC 251 (Justice Belobaba); $750M Settlement Agreement March 2018; Patrick Johnston "Native Children and the Child Welfare System" 1983; Senate Standing Committee on Human Rights "The Welfare of Indigenous Children" 2017; Métis Nation of Ontario Sixties Scoop settlement 2021.

Section 5 — Boil Water Advisories: The Broken Promise
Boil Water Advisories — Trudeau's 2015 Promise: Still 28 Communities Without Safe Water in 2024
BROKEN PROMISE Federal Liberal 2015–2024 ETHICS 95% [NPU]

During the 2015 federal election campaign, Justin Trudeau pledged to end all long-term drinking water advisories on First Nations reserves "within five years." The deadline of November 2020 passed with dozens of communities still affected. The government revised the target multiple times. As of early 2024, 28 communities remain under long-term advisories (down from 105 in 2015) — meaning the government lifted approximately 74% of advisories but has not fulfilled the original promise. Some communities that had advisories lifted had new ones imposed due to infrastructure failures.

The federal government announced $1.8B for water infrastructure in 2016 and additional funds in subsequent budgets. Infrastructure Canada and ISC report ongoing capacity and technical challenges. Critics including the Assembly of First Nations point to persistent underfunding of operations and maintenance, lack of trained operators in remote communities, and failure to consult communities on infrastructure design as reasons for the shortfall. Many communities have lived under boil water advisories for over a decade.

The AG found "minimal improvement" in the department's capacity to address long-term issues since a 2005 audit.

Section 6 — UNDRIP, Free Prior Informed Consent, and Infrastructure Overrides
UNDRIP in Canadian Law — Bill C-15 (2021): Passed Without Enforcement; Consultation Gaps Persist
SYSTEMIC FAILURE Federal Liberal 2021–2024 ETHICS 90% [NPU]

Bill C-15, the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act, received Royal Assent June 21, 2021, making Canada the first G7 country to enshrine UNDRIP principles in domestic law. The Act requires the federal government to develop an action plan in consultation with Indigenous peoples, and to ensure federal laws are consistent with UNDRIP. The National Action Plan was released June 2023 — one year late.

Key principle: Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) — the right of Indigenous peoples to be consulted and to give or withhold consent for projects affecting their territories. Critics including the Assembly of First Nations and Indigenous rights scholars note that Bill C-15 does not create a veto right, contains no enforcement mechanism, and does not require FPIC to be respected in practice — only "consultation." Two major infrastructure projects illustrate the gap between the law's promise and its application.

Case Studies: Infrastructure and Consent

Trans Mountain Pipeline Expansion — Government Overrode Indigenous Consultation Concerns; $34B Federal Investment
The Trans Mountain Expansion Project (TMX) — twinning the existing pipeline from Alberta to Burnaby, BC — was purchased by the federal government in August 2018 for $4.5B after Kinder Morgan threatened to cancel it. Total federal investment exceeded $34B by 2024. Multiple First Nations in BC filed legal challenges arguing the duty to consult was inadequately fulfilled. The Federal Court of Appeal found in 2018 that consultation was "inadequate in a number of critical ways" and quashed the original approval. The government re-did consultations and re-approved the project. Further legal challenges from First Nations (Secwépemc, Stk'emlúpsemc te Secwépemc, Cold Lake First Nations) continued through to 2024. The pipeline became operational in 2024 despite ongoing objections from several First Nations along the route who stated their land, water, and treaty rights had not been respected. The government justified the project on national economic interest and energy security grounds.
Site C Dam (BC) — Treaty Rights Violations Alleged; $16B Project Proceeded Over Indigenous Objections
The Site C Clean Energy Project — a BC Hydro dam on the Peace River in Treaty 8 territory — was approved by the BC government in 2014 and remained a federal regulatory approval. Treaty 8 First Nations (Prophet River, West Moberly, Blueberry River) argued their treaty rights to hunt, fish and trap on affected lands were violated without adequate consultation or compensation. The BC Utilities Commission found in 2017 that the project was not the most cost-effective energy option and warned of budget overruns. The BC NDP government nonetheless proceeded in 2017, citing sunk costs. Total cost ballooned from $8.8B to over $16B. As of 2024 the dam is operational; ongoing litigation from Treaty 8 nations continues. The Blueberry River First Nations reached a $200M government settlement in 2023 over broader cumulative impacts on their treaty territory — partially addressing Site C concerns, but not reversing the project.
"UNDRIP does not mean that Indigenous peoples have a veto over projects. Let me be clear about that." — Crown-Indigenous Relations Minister Carolyn Bennett, House of Commons, February 17, 2016 (re: UNDRIP)
"The government of Canada's position is fundamentally inconsistent with the free, prior and informed consent standard of UNDRIP as it was understood when it was adopted." — Assembly of First Nations submission to House Committee on Bill C-15, April 2021

Further Reading — Systemic Accountability
Summary: What Has and Has Not Been Done
ACCOUNTABILITY SUMMARY 2015–2024 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Canada has offered formal apologies (2008 residential schools; 2017 Sixties Scoop), passed landmark legislation (Bill C-15 UNDRIP; Bill C-91 Languages; Bill C-92 child welfare jurisdiction), and entered into $40B+ in compensation settlements. Courts and tribunals have repeatedly found the federal government in discrimination and contempt for failing to protect Indigenous rights and children.

What remains undone: a national monument to residential school survivors (Call #80, after 10 years); mandatory K–12 residential schools curriculum across all provinces (Call #62); full implementation of Jordan's Principle without ongoing legal battles; an independent RCMP oversight body for MMIWG cases; and safe drinking water for all 28 remaining communities. The government's own National Council for Reconciliation — mandated by TRC Call #53, passed into law via Bill C-29 in 2023 — has not yet released an annual accountability report. The gap between legislative commitments and lived reality remains the central accountability issue in Canadian indigenous policy.

Sources: National Council for Reconciliation Act (Bill C-29, 2023); AFN Implementation Tracking 2024; Yellowhead Institute TRC Tracker; ISC boil water advisory database; CHRT ongoing compliance hearings 2024.
