National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls (MMIWG) — Genocide Finding
GENOCIDE FINDING Federal 2016–2019 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

The National Inquiry into MMIWG, mandated in September 2016 and reporting June 3, 2019, heard testimony from more than 2,380 family members, survivors, experts and knowledge keepers over two and a half years. The Final Report — Reclaiming Power and Place — concluded that the violence against Indigenous women and girls constitutes a "race-based genocide" under international law, carried out through colonial policies and ongoing state and institutional failures.

The inquiry found the RCMP's own 2014 report (which identified 1,181 cases between 1980–2012) was itself an undercount, and that systemic RCMP failures — including inadequate investigation, dismissal of cases, and harassment of families seeking answers — constituted direct contributions to ongoing violence. The RCMP's National Centre for Missing Persons and Unidentified Remains (NCMPUR) lacked resources and coordination throughout the inquiry period.

"We are calling on the federal government to immediately recognize and implement the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) and implement the TRC's Calls to Action." — MMIWG Final Report, Vol. 1a, Calls for Justice 1.1, June 2019
"The violence the National Inquiry heard about amounts to a race-based genocide of Indigenous Peoples, including First Nations, Métis and Inuit, carried out by the Canadian state." — MMIWG Final Report, "A Legal Analysis of Genocide," p. 50

Government Response: Prime Minister Trudeau accepted the Final Report June 3, 2019, and pledged to develop a National Action Plan. The National Action Plan was released June 2021 — two years after the report. As of 2024, advocates and the inquiry's own commissioners have stated the Action Plan is insufficiently funded and lacks binding implementation mechanisms. The RCMP has not been subject to independent oversight reform as called for in the report.

Sources: MMIWG Final Report "Reclaiming Power and Place," June 3, 2019; RCMP Missing and Murdered Aboriginal Women Report 2014; National Action Plan in Response to the MMIWG Final Report, June 2021; Native Women's Association of Canada implementation tracking 2022–2024.

Truth and Reconciliation Commission — 94 Calls to Action: Implementation Status
SYSTEMIC FAILURE Federal + Provincial 2015–2024 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada (TRC), chaired by Justice Murray Sinclair, released its Final Report and 94 Calls to Action in December 2015. The Commission documented the experiences of survivors of the Indian Residential School System — 139 federally funded schools where an estimated 150,000 Indigenous children were forcibly separated from families between the 1870s and 1996. The TRC estimated at least 4,100 children died in the schools; the National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation now estimates 6,000+. Causes included disease, physical abuse, malnutrition, and neglect.

Reconciliation Canada and the Yellowhead Institute's annual tracking (2023) found only 13 of 94 Calls fully completed, 43 in progress, and 38 not started or stalled. Key unfulfilled calls include: a national action plan on missing children and burial information (Call 75), full implementation of UNDRIP (Call 43), and adequate, stable funding for Indigenous languages (Calls 13–15).

Jordan's Principle & CHRT — Canada Found in Contempt; $40B Compensation Order
DISCRIMINATION Federal Liberal 2007–2023 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Jordan's Principle — named for Jordan River Anderson, a First Nations child from Norway House Cree Nation who died in hospital in 2005 while the federal and provincial governments disputed who would pay for his home care — requires the government of first contact to pay for services First Nations children need without delay. The Canadian Human Rights Tribunal (CHRT) first found Canada discriminatory in its underfunding of on-reserve child and family services in 2016. Canada appealed and lost repeatedly.

In 2019, the CHRT found Canada in contempt for failing to comply with the 2016 order. In 2022, after years of litigation, Canada negotiated a settlement in principle: $20B in compensation to First Nations children and families harmed by the discriminatory child welfare system since 1991, plus $20B for long-term reform — totalling $40B. The settlement required court approval; as of 2024 implementation is ongoing and advocates report ongoing delays in individual payments.

"Canada has been wilfully and recklessly discriminating against 165,000 First Nations children for years. The government spent more money fighting Indigenous children in court than it would have cost to simply provide them the services they needed." — Cindy Blackstock, First Nations Child & Family Caring Society, 2019

Sources: CHRT Decision 2016 CHRT 2 (First Nations Child and Family Caring Society v. Attorney General of Canada); CHRT Contempt Finding 2019 CHRT 39; $40B settlement agreement in principle August 2022; Federal Court approval process 2023; Cindy Blackstock parliamentary testimony 2019.

Sixties Scoop — $750M Federal Settlement; Intergenerational Trauma
SYSTEMIC FAILURE Federal 1960s–1990s | Settlement 2018 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Between the 1960s and 1980s, the federal government facilitated the mass removal of Indigenous children from their families and communities and placed them with non-Indigenous families — largely without consent and in violation of First Nations jurisdiction over child welfare. An estimated 20,000 Indigenous children were removed in Canada, many sent to the United States, Australia and Europe. Children lost their languages, cultures, and connections to family and community. Many reported physical and sexual abuse in adoptive homes.

In 2017, Ontario Superior Court Justice Edward Belobaba certified a class action; in 2018 the federal government agreed to a $750M settlement covering approximately 22,000 eligible claimants. The settlement was criticized by some survivors as insufficient. Many First Nations groups noted the settlement did not include Métis and Inuit children, though subsequent settlements addressed some of these groups.

Sources: Brown v. Canada (Attorney General), 2017 ONSC 251; $750M Settlement Agreement 2018; Auditor General's report on Indigenous child welfare 2018; Senate Standing Committee on Human Rights "The Welfare of Indigenous Children" 2017.
