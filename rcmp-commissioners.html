Giuliano Zaccardelli
August 2000 — December 6, 2006
Chr. (Conservative) → Martin (Liberal) RESIGNED UNDER PRESSURE

Appointment

Appointed by Prime Minister Jean Chrétien via Order-in-Council, August 2000. Reappointed. Served under both Liberal governments (Chrétien, Martin) and the beginning of Harper's Conservative government.

OIC 2000-1312 (approx.) | Served under: Chrétien, Martin, Harper (briefly)

The Maher Arar Affair

Under Zaccardelli's command, the RCMP provided false and inaccurate information to the US government describing Maher Arar — a Canadian-Syrian dual citizen — as an Islamic extremist. The US detained Arar during a transit stop and rendered him to Syria, where he was tortured for approximately one year. The O'Connor Commission (Justice Dennis O'Connor, 2006) found the RCMP information was "inaccurate, portrayed him in an unfair light and was communicated without any consideration of the consequences."

Parliamentary Testimony Contradiction

Zaccardelli testified before the House of Commons Public Safety Committee in September 2006 that he first learned of the inaccurate RCMP information sent to US authorities in 2006. He returned to testify in November 2006 and admitted he had actually known since 2002 — contradicting his earlier testimony. This is functionally perjury before Parliament, though he was never charged. He resigned December 6, 2006.

Accountability Verdict

Resigned following the admission of false parliamentary testimony. Never charged. The RCMP's false information directly caused Arar's rendition and torture. Canada paid Arar $10.5 million in compensation. Zaccardelli faced no criminal accountability.

William Elliott
July 2007 — November 2011
Harper (Conservative) First civilian Commissioner

Appointment

Appointed by Prime Minister Stephen Harper in 2007 — the first civilian ever appointed RCMP Commissioner. A former senior public servant (Associate Deputy Minister of Public Safety). The appointment was controversial within the force, which had traditionally promoted from within.

OIC 2007-1060 | Appointed by: Harper (Conservative)

Tenure Notes

Elliott's tenure was marked by internal tensions; senior RCMP officers anonymously complained to media about his management style, describing a "toxic" work environment. A review by former Ontario AG David Brown found "serious morale problems." Elliott departed November 2011 with no formal resignation or public scandal but amid ongoing cultural reform challenges within the RCMP.

Accountability Verdict

No criminal or parliamentary accountability findings. Internal management concerns documented. No significant institutional failures attributed directly during his tenure. Departed quietly.

Bob Paulson
November 2011 — June 2017
Harper (Conservative) → Trudeau (Liberal) BILL C-14 PERIOD

Appointment

Appointed by Prime Minister Stephen Harper in November 2011. Continued under Prime Minister Justin Trudeau (elected October 2015). He was Commissioner when Bill C-14 — Canada's original MAID legislation — passed Parliament in June 2016, establishing the legal framework for state-assisted death.

OIC 2011-1366 | Appointed by: Harper | Continued under: Trudeau

MAID Watch

Paulson was RCMP Commissioner for the entire legislative history of Bill C-14 and the first operational year of MAID. In 2016, 1,018 Canadians died under MAID provisions. Disability advocacy groups had raised Charter concerns about the bill's eligibility safeguards during this period. The RCMP under Paulson took no public position on MAID's legal safeguards and opened no related investigations.

Other Issues

Paulson's tenure included significant RCMP harassment scandals — a 2016 class action settlement ($100 million) over systemic sexual harassment within the RCMP was reached under his command, though the harassment itself predated him.

Accountability Verdict

No personal criminal or parliamentary accountability findings. Oversaw MAID's introduction without engaging on safeguard questions. Retired June 2017 with no public statement on MAID, disability rights concerns, or his role in the first year of the regime.

Brenda Lucki
March 2018 — September 2023
Trudeau (Liberal) PRIMARY ACCOUNTABILITY COMMISSIONER FOUND: POLITICAL INTERFERENCE

Appointment

Appointed by Prime Minister Justin Trudeau via Order-in-Council, March 2018. The first woman to serve as RCMP Commissioner. Her appointment came after an acting period by Interim Commissioner Gilles Moreau. She was directly appointed by and reported to the same government that was simultaneously driving the MAID expansion agenda.

OIC 2018-0438 | Appointed by: Trudeau (Liberal)

MAID Expansion — The Full Watch Period

Lucki commanded the RCMP through the single most consequential period of MAID expansion. Under her tenure: Bill C-7 passed (March 17, 2021), removing the "reasonably foreseeable natural death" requirement and opening Track 2 for non-terminal patients. Annual MAID deaths grew from 2,838 (2017) to 13,241 (2022) — a 367% increase. Multiple documented cases of safeguard failures emerged publicly (Roger Foley, Alan Nichols, the Veterans Affairs incident). UN Special Rapporteurs sent formal communications to Canada expressing concern. The RCMP under Lucki initiated zero investigations into MAID safeguard compliance.

Nova Scotia Mass Shooting — Political Interference Finding

The Mass Casualty Commission (Final Report, March 2023) found that Commissioner Lucki told senior RCMP officers — including Nova Scotia RCMP Commanding Officer Lee Bergerman and Chief Superintendent Darren Campbell — that she had promised Minister of Public Safety Bill Blair and the Prime Minister's Office that the RCMP would release information about the firearms used in the April 2020 Nova Scotia mass shooting (22 victims) in a way that would support the federal government's gun control legislative agenda. Chief Supt. Campbell recorded notes of the meeting contemporaneously. Lucki acknowledged the meeting but denied making a "promise." The Commission found her account less credible than Campbell's contemporaneous notes.

Resignation

Lucki announced her resignation in September 2023. The announcement followed the tabling of the Mass Casualty Commission report and years of public criticism over her handling of both the shooting investigation and RCMP reform.

Accountability Verdict — Primary Finding

The Mass Casualty Commission found Lucki compromised RCMP independence to serve the political interests of the Trudeau government. She simultaneously presided over the largest MAID expansion in Canadian history without initiating a single investigation into documented safeguard failures. Her tenure represents the clearest intersection of RCMP political capture and institutional failure to protect vulnerable Canadians during this period.

Mike Duheme
September 2023 — Present
Trudeau (Liberal) → Carney (Liberal) CURRENT COMMISSIONER

Appointment

Appointed by Prime Minister Justin Trudeau via Order-in-Council, September 2023, following Lucki's resignation. Former Deputy Commissioner. Career RCMP officer. Has continued under Prime Minister Mark Carney following the 2025 federal election.

OIC 2023-0887 | Appointed by: Trudeau | Continuing under: Carney

MAID — Ongoing Watch

Duheme assumed command as the MAID regime reached approximately 15,000+ deaths per year. The MAID-MD-SUMC expansion (mental illness as sole condition) has been delayed multiple times — a Senate report acknowledged the system was "not ready." No investigation into Track 2 safeguard compliance, the documented housing/poverty cases, or the VAC incident has been announced under Duheme's tenure as of 2025.

Current Status

Too early for a comprehensive accountability verdict. No major public scandal as of publication. The foundational question — whether the RCMP under Duheme will investigate documented MAID safeguard failures — remains unanswered.
