The NSICOP Report — June 2024

⚠
What is NSICOP? The National Security and Intelligence Committee of Parliamentarians is a committee of MPs and Senators with top-secret security clearance that reviews classified national security and intelligence files. Unlike most committees, it sees raw CSIS and CSE intelligence. Its reports are reviewed by the PM before public release — sections may be redacted.
Core Conclusion
Foreign interference is "an enduring and escalating threat to Canadian democracy." The report found that interference activities are not isolated incidents but a systematic, multi-year campaign by several foreign states with long-term strategic objectives inside Canada.
MPs Found Complicit
Several unnamed Members of Parliament were found to have "wittingly or unwittingly" assisted foreign states. NSICOP concluded some MPs actively passed information to foreign missions, attended foreign government-funded events, and provided access in exchange for electoral support. Names are classified and were not publicly released.
CSIS Briefings Ignored
CSIS briefed the Prime Minister, the Clerk of the Privy Council, and senior officials on foreign interference on multiple occasions. The report found that government action in response was minimal and that the intelligence was not acted upon at the level the threat demanded.
Liberal Nomination Races Targeted
CSIS warned the Liberal Party of Canada that at least one nomination race had been targeted by foreign state interference — with foreign consulate officials directing funds and community organizers to influence the outcome. The warning was received and ignored.
Foreign States Named
The report explicitly names: People's Republic of China (PRC), India, Pakistan, and Iran as conducting foreign interference operations targeting Canada's political system, diaspora communities, and democratic institutions.
"The government treated foreign interference as a partisan political issue rather than a national security threat."
— NSICOP Report, June 2024

The Hogue Commission — Public Inquiry 2023–2024

ℹ
The Commission: Justice Marie-Josée Hogue of the Quebec Court of Appeal was appointed to lead the Public Inquiry into Foreign Interference in Federal Electoral Processes and Democratic Institutions. The inquiry held public hearings across 2023–2024, heard from CSIS, RCMP, PMO, PCO, political party officials, academics, and diaspora community members. Its mandate covered the 2019 and 2021 elections.
Core Finding

"Foreign interference activities did occur" — in both elections

Justice Hogue concluded that foreign interference activities took place during both the 2019 and 2021 federal elections. While she stopped short of saying the overall election results were illegitimate, she found specific ridings were materially affected and that the total impact may never be fully measurable.

PRC Strategic Objective

Prevent Conservative government — sustain Liberal/NDP power

Beijing's documented goal was to prevent a Conservative majority or minority government, which it assessed as more hawkish on Taiwan, Hong Kong, and human rights. PRC preferred the Liberals and viewed the NDP under Singh as more favourable. Interference was calibrated toward this end.

11 Ridings — 2021 Election

Targeted ridings with large Chinese-Canadian diaspora populations

CSIS identified 11 ridings in the 2021 election that were specifically targeted by PRC interference operations. These were predominantly urban ridings in the Greater Toronto Area and Metro Vancouver with large Chinese-Canadian communities. PRC consulate officials were directed to mobilize diaspora voters and fund preferred candidates' campaigns indirectly.

Han Dong — Don Valley North (Liberal)

CSIS concluded he had undisclosed relationship with Chinese officials

CSIS assessed that Han Dong had an undisclosed relationship with People's Republic of China officials and that he passed information to them. He reportedly advised PRC consulate officials on how to handle detained Canadians Michael Kovrig and Michael Spavor. Han Dong denied the allegations and resigned from the Liberal caucus in March 2023, sitting as an independent. He also sued Global News for defamation. He did not recontest his seat in the 2025 election.

Michael Chan — Ontario Politics

CSIS flagged repeated concerns about PRC contacts

Michael Chan, former Ontario cabinet minister and York Region politician, was repeatedly flagged by CSIS for his alleged ties to PRC officials and the Toronto consulate. CSIS concerns dated back years and were reportedly raised with Ontario Liberal leadership. Chan denied any improper conduct. The commission heard testimony regarding his role in PRC-connected diaspora networks.

WeChat Operations

Pro-Beijing messaging targeting Chinese-Canadian communities

Evidence before the commission documented coordinated WeChat campaigns targeting Chinese-Canadian diaspora groups. Pro-Beijing messaging amplified support for specific Liberal candidates while spreading disinformation about Conservative candidates, including false claims about racism and anti-Chinese policies. WeChat's server infrastructure in China enables PRC censorship and monitoring of diaspora communities.

China's Operation — PRC Diaspora Mobilization

Overview

The People's Republic of China ran the most sophisticated and documented foreign interference operation in Canadian electoral history. It combined diplomatic networks (consulates), PRC-linked community organizations, social media (particularly WeChat), diaspora pressure, candidate funding, and intelligence collection — all coordinated toward preventing a Conservative government and maintaining preferred policy positions on Taiwan, Tibet, Hong Kong, and bilateral trade.

Consulate Direction to Mobilize Diaspora
PRC consulates in Toronto and Vancouver were documented directing community liaisons to mobilize Chinese-Canadian voters for preferred candidates. CSIS intercepts included consulate officials coordinating bussing of voters, attending events for targeted candidates, and tracking electoral outcomes riding by riding.
Indirect Campaign Finance
Evidence presented to Hogue documented PRC-linked networks providing indirect financial support to preferred candidates through community fundraising events, donations routed through intermediaries, and in-kind support. This circumvented Canada Elections Act rules on foreign national donations.
Intelligence Collection on Diaspora
PRC consulates maintained surveillance of Chinese-Canadian community members, tracking political activity, donations, and loyalty. Community members with family in China were pressured to support PRC objectives. CSIS documented coercive tactics including threats against family members in China.
2019 Election: Nomination Targeting
In the 2019 election cycle, PRC-linked operatives interfered in Liberal nomination races to ensure preferred candidates won nominations in key ridings. CSIS warned the Liberal Party — the warning was received but no corrective action was taken to investigate or disqualify affected candidates.

India's Operation — Sikh Diaspora Surveillance & Political Pressure

⚠
State-Sanctioned Murder on Canadian Soil: On June 18, 2023, Hardeep Singh Nijjar — a Canadian citizen and Sikh community leader in Surrey, BC — was shot dead in the parking lot of the Guru Nanak Sikh Gurdwara. CSIS and the RCMP concluded that agents of the Indian government were responsible. This is the most serious confirmed act of foreign state violence on Canadian territory in modern history.
Nijjar Assassination

Hardeep Singh Nijjar, 45, was designated by India as a "terrorist" for his advocacy of a Sikh homeland (Khalistan). CSIS had intelligence indicating Indian government involvement and briefed PM Trudeau before Trudeau's public statement in Parliament on September 18, 2023, accusing India of "potential" involvement.

India denied any involvement and expelled Canadian diplomats in retaliation. Canada expelled Indian High Commissioner Sanjay Kumar Verma and several Indian diplomats. Three Indian nationals were charged by the RCMP with first-degree murder in 2023. India continued to deny state involvement.

Confirmed: State actor responsible 3 charged with murder
Sikh Diaspora Surveillance

NSICOP found that India ran systematic surveillance of Sikh-Canadians, particularly those with Khalistan sympathies. Indian consulates in Canada monitored community members, reported on their activities to New Delhi, and used informants inside community organizations.

CSIS assessed that community members were intimidated, surveilled, and in some cases threatened with consequences to family members in India. The surveillance extended to political activities, donations to advocacy groups, and attendance at community events.

Political Candidate Influence

NSICOP and CSIS flagged concerns about Indian government ties to nominated candidates in multiple parties, including Conservative candidates with alleged connections to Indian consulate networks. India's goal was to ensure no pro-Khalistan or India-critical politicians gained power.

Prabmeet Sarkaria (Ontario PC cabinet minister) was among names CSIS flagged for Indian government connections. Reports indicated Indian consulate officials tracked his career and maintained contact with his network.

What Trudeau Knew — and When

⚠
The Central Question: Did the Prime Minister and his senior staff receive intelligence warnings about foreign interference and fail to act? The answer, established by sworn testimony before the Hogue Commission and NSICOP's classified review, is yes. The question of why remains contested — incompetence, partisanship, or both.
2019
CSIS warns Liberal Party about nomination interference
CSIS briefed Liberal Party officials that at least one nomination race had been targeted by PRC operatives. No investigation was conducted. The affected nomination proceeded.
Oct 2019
2019 federal election — interference documented but not disclosed
Hogue Commission found interference occurred in 2019. Trudeau government did not publicly acknowledge the extent of foreign interference during or after the election.
2021
CSIS briefs PM Trudeau on PRC interference — pre-election
Multiple CSIS briefings to the PM and Privy Council Office documented the scope of PRC operations. The National Security and Intelligence Adviser (NSIA) flagged concerns. Trudeau received and acknowledged the briefings. No action was taken before the election.
Sep 2021
2021 federal election — 11 ridings actively targeted
Election proceeds. CSIS documents real-time interference in 11 ridings. PRC consulate officials are operationally active. Results in several ridings are assessed as potentially affected. Government wins minority. No public disclosure follows.
2022
Global News & Globe and Mail report CSIS intelligence
Investigative reporting forces the government's hand. Reports detail PRC targeting of ridings, Han Dong allegations, and the government's failure to act. Trudeau government initially dismisses reports as "inaccurate."
2022
Katie Telford (Chief of Staff) testifies — she knew
Under oath before a parliamentary committee, Chief of Staff Katie Telford confirmed she was aware of CSIS intelligence on foreign interference. She maintained the government acted appropriately. Critics noted that no meaningful action was documented.
Mar 2023
David Johnston appointed Special Rapporteur — then resigns
Former Governor General David Johnston was appointed to review foreign interference. Critics immediately noted Johnston's connections to the Desmarais family (Power Corporation), major Liberal donors, whose foundation had employed him. Johnston initially recommended against a public inquiry. He resigned in June 2023 after sustained conflict-of-interest criticism.
Sep 2023

After Johnston's resignation and sustained political pressure, PM Trudeau announced Justice Hogue would lead a full public inquiry. The inquiry's terms of reference covered 2019 and 2021 elections.
Jun 2024
NSICOP report released — MPs found complicit
NSICOP's classified assessment is made public (partially). Multiple unnamed MPs found to have assisted foreign states. CSIS director David Vigneault calls interference an "existential threat" in testimony.
"Some members of Parliament — whether wittingly or unwittingly — were, or attempted to be, cultivated by foreign states, and some were directly involved in activities that advanced the interests of a foreign state."

Bill C-70 — The Legislative Response (2024)

What Bill C-70 Does

✓ Foreign Influence Transparency Registry

Individuals and organizations acting on behalf of foreign principals in relation to political or governmental processes must register. Modelled on the US FARA system.

✓ New Criminal Offences for Foreign Interference

Creates specific Criminal Code offences for clandestine or deceptive conduct at the direction of a foreign principal to influence democratic processes. Maximum 14-year sentences.

✓ CSIS Information-Sharing Expanded

Expands CSIS's ability to share threat information with a broader range of entities including political parties and candidates — addressing the gap where CSIS knew but couldn't warn directly.

✓ Passed with Broad Support

Bill C-70 passed the House of Commons with support from Liberals, Conservatives, NDP, and Bloc — unusually rapid passage signalling cross-party consensus on the threat.

Criticisms & Gaps

✗ Broad Exemptions in Registry

Unlike the US FARA, C-70's registry contains numerous exemptions — including for academic, cultural, and religious activities — that critics argue create large loopholes for exactly the channels PRC uses for interference.

✗ No Retroactive Accountability

The legislation does not address past conduct. MPs identified in NSICOP's report face no legal consequence under the new law. Documented interference from 2019 and 2021 receives no criminal accountability.

✗ Enforcement Mechanism Weak

Critics note the registry relies on self-disclosure, with enforcement dependent on RCMP investigation capacity and political will to prosecute. The RCMP has historically been under-resourced for foreign interference investigations.

✗ No Diaspora Coercion Remedy

The bill does not create specific protections or remedies for diaspora Canadians who are coerced, surveilled, or intimidated by foreign state actors — the most pervasive form of interference documented by CSIS.

Criminal Code Analysis — Applicable Provisions

ℹ
Note: The following is a summary of existing Criminal Code provisions potentially applicable to the documented conduct. As of 2024, no charges have been laid against any Canadian political figure for foreign interference-related conduct. Whether the evidence meets criminal standards beyond reasonable doubt is a legal determination for prosecutors. This is public legal analysis.
Section 46 — Treason
Criminal Code of Canada, RSC 1985, c C-46, s 46
A person commits treason who communicates, or makes available to an agent of a state other than Canada, military or scientific information or any sketch, plan, model, article, note or document of a nature that he knows or ought to know may be used by that state for a purpose prejudicial to the safety or defence of Canada. Also applies to one who conspires with a foreign state to commit acts intended to cause Canada embarrassment or to assist a foreign state in military operations against Canada.
Potential application: NSICOP found MPs passed information to foreign state officials. If that information was of a nature prejudicial to Canada's security or democratic processes, s.46 is potentially applicable. Prosecution would require proving the accused knew the purpose was prejudicial. Maximum sentence: 14 years.
Section 16 — High Treason
Criminal Code of Canada, RSC 1985, c C-46, s 16
Every one commits high treason who, in Canada, levies war against Canada or does any act preparatory thereto, or assists an enemy at war with Canada, or delivers Canada or any of its armed forces to a foreign power, or uses force or violence to overthrow the government of Canada, a province, or territory, or conspires with any person to do any of those things.
Potential application: Less directly applicable to the documented conduct than s.46 unless the foreign interference was designed to overthrow or fundamentally alter the outcome of democratic government by force or threat. The documented operations — while severe — fall primarily under s.46, not high treason. Maximum sentence: Life imprisonment.
Section 282.1–282.5 — Foreign Interference (Bill C-70, 2024)
Criminal Code of Canada, as amended by An Act respecting foreign interference (2024)
New offences created by Bill C-70 include: (a) committing an act of foreign interference — clandestine or deceptive conduct, at the direction of or in association with a foreign state, intended to influence a democratic process, impede law enforcement, or harm a person for exercising democratic rights; (b) registrable conduct before registration.
Potential application: Directly applicable to documented conduct including WeChat disinformation campaigns, consulate-directed voter mobilization, and any future documented clandestine foreign principal relationships with political figures. Not retroactive to pre-2024 conduct. Maximum: 14 years.
Section 423.1 — Intimidation of Justice System Participants
Criminal Code of Canada, RSC 1985, c C-46, s 423.1
Prohibits conduct intended to provoke a state of fear in a person associated with the justice system, government, or in a journalist, for the purpose of impeding them in the performance of their duties or retaliating against them. Applies to threats and intimidation.
Potential application: Indian government surveillance and intimidation of Sikh-Canadians, including threats to their family members in India, could constitute intimidation of persons exercising Canadian rights. The Nijjar assassination is first-degree murder under s.235 — charges were laid against three individuals but not against Indian state officials.
Canada Elections Act — Foreign National Contributions
Canada Elections Act, SC 2000, c 9, s 331
It is an offence for a foreign national to make a contribution to a registered party, electoral district association, candidate, leadership contestant, or nomination contestant. It is also an offence to circumvent this prohibition by using Canadian intermediaries to funnel foreign funds.
Potential application: Evidence before Hogue of PRC-linked indirect financial support to candidates could constitute offences under s.331 and related provisions. Elections Canada and the Commissioner of Canada Elections have investigatory authority. No charges have been laid related to documented PRC-linked campaign finance interference.

Why This Matters — And What Must Happen

Foreign states are operating inside Canada's democratic system — targeting diaspora communities, cultivating MPs, funding candidates, and assassinating critics on Canadian soil. The documented government response has been denial, delay, and minimal action. Accountability requires informed citizens demanding it.

  • Full public release of NSICOP findings including the names of compromised MPs
  • RCMP criminal investigation into documented s.46 treason and campaign finance offences
  • Strengthening of Bill C-70 to remove exemptions and add retroactive provisions
  • Mandatory CSIS briefings to all party leaders within 30 days of identifying threats to MPs
  • Independent review of all federal nomination contests in targeted ridings (2015–2023)
  • Sanctions and visa restrictions on Indian and PRC officials identified in interference operations
  • Full diplomatic accountability for the Nijjar assassination — including ICC referral consideration
