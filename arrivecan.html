INVESTIGATIVE RECORD NOTICE: All figures, findings, and quotes on this page are drawn exclusively from official sources: the Auditor General of Canada's 2023 Special Report, House of Commons Standing Committee on Government Operations and Estimates (OGGO) testimony transcripts, CBSA public disclosures, and parliamentary debate records. Readers are strongly encouraged to consult primary sources. An RCMP investigation is ongoing; no charges have been publicly announced as of the date of last update.

In March 2020, the Canada Border Services Agency received an $80,000 quote to build a simple travel-tracking application for pandemic border control. Twenty-four months later, the same app — beset by 177 known bugs at launch and responsible for the wrongful quarantine of approximately 10,000 Canadians — had consumed $59.5 million in public funds. The principal contractor was a 2-person company operating out of a residential address with no permanent staff, no technical expertise, and no competitive procurement process. The Auditor General of Canada called it "the worst example of pandemic contracting" her office had ever seen. No minister has accepted responsibility. No charges have been laid. This is the documented record.

02The Contract — From $80,000 to $59.5 Million

The ArriveCAN application was conceived in March 2020 as an emergency measure to collect traveller health declarations at the border during the early COVID-19 pandemic. CBSA staff obtained a preliminary quote of approximately $80,000 for a basic digital form. What followed was a two-year procurement free-for-all in which the CBSA entered into contracts with GCStrategies — a company with two employees and no technological capabilities — without competitive bidding, relying on emergency exception clauses intended for genuine one-time emergencies, not ongoing multi-year projects.

03GCStrategies — Who Are They?

GCStrategies is a small Ottawa-based consulting firm incorporated in 2015. At the time of the ArriveCAN contracts, it employed exactly two people: co-founders Kristian Firth and Darren Anthony. The company had no software developers, no engineers, no quality assurance staff, and no office beyond a residential address. Its business model was explicitly intermediary: match government contracts to subcontractors, take a management fee from the top. What distinguished GCStrategies in the ArriveCAN scandal was the scale at which this model was applied — and the absence of any due diligence by CBSA in awarding it tens of millions of public dollars.

Kristian Firth
Co-Founder & Principal, GCStrategies
REFUSED TESTIMONY

Firth appeared before the OGGO parliamentary committee in 2023 and repeatedly invoked his right not to answer questions about the identities of subcontractors used in ArriveCAN. He confirmed GCStrategies had no technical staff and that the company's role was to "manage the talent." He acknowledged the company kept a commission on billings but declined to specify the rate. Was found in contempt of Parliament following continued non-compliance with committee orders to produce documents.

Darren Anthony
Co-Founder & Principal, GCStrategies
REFUSED TESTIMONY

Anthony co-appeared at committee hearings alongside Firth and similarly refused to provide full disclosure of the subcontractor chain. Both principals initially declined to appear when first summoned. When they did appear, their answers were characterized by MPs as evasive and non-responsive. Anthony, like Firth, maintained that the company provided legitimate talent-matching services to the government.

In numerous cases, CBSA could not produce invoices, statements of work, or deliverable records to justify payments already made. O'Gorman became CBSA President in 2022 and appeared before OGGO to answer for ArriveCAN. She acknowledged systemic oversight failures within CBSA but maintained that corrective actions were underway. Critics noted that her testimony offered little accountability for the officials who approved the contracts, many of whom remained employed by the agency.

04The Subcontractor Maze

GCStrategies did not build the ArriveCAN app. It subcontracted the actual work to other firms and individuals, who in turn subcontracted to others — creating chains of up to four layers deep before public money reached an actual developer. At each layer, a percentage was retained as a management or brokering fee. The Auditor General found that this layered structure made it impossible to fully audit the use of public funds.

  • 🔗 No competitive subcontracting: GCStrategies selected subcontractors without a competitive process, contrary to Treasury Board guidelines for government-funded projects at this scale.
  • 🔗 Identity concealment: GCStrategies principals refused in parliamentary testimony to name the individuals and companies who received public money as sub and sub-subcontractors.
  • 🔗 Rate markup undisclosed: The actual markup percentage retained at each layer was not disclosed to Parliament or the Auditor General with full transparency. AG analysis suggests management fees consumed a significant portion of each contract value.
  • 🔗 Accenture involvement: Parliamentary testimony revealed that Accenture — a major IT consulting firm — was also involved in ArriveCAN development, receiving contracts directly and through intermediaries. The full scope of Accenture's billings drew separate scrutiny from the committee.
  • 🔗 Phantom deliverables: CBSA paid for work it could not document as received. The AG found cases where payments were made without statements of work defining what was to be delivered.

05The App Failures — 177 Bugs, 10,000 False Quarantines

For an app that cost $59.5 million and was backed by the coercive power of federal border enforcement — including mandatory fines and quarantine orders — ArriveCAN was functionally defective from the moment of its public release. CBSA's own internal testing team documented 177 known bugs before launch. The government deployed it anyway. The consequences were not abstract: real Canadians were subjected to mandatory quarantine orders they should not have received.

Documented Defects — ArriveCAN at Launch

  • 177 known bugs documented by CBSA testing team prior to mandatory enforcement launch — app deployed regardless.
  • ~10,000 travellers incorrectly ordered into mandatory quarantine due to app logic errors. Many had complied with all requirements; the app erroneously flagged them as non-compliant.
  • Vaccination status errors: App failed to correctly recognize or retain vaccination certificate data for some users, triggering false non-compliance flags.
  • Session timeout data loss: Users who took too long to complete submission lost all entered data and received error responses that could be mistaken for compliance failures.
  • Platform-specific failures: Certain Android versions and older iOS versions experienced submission failures with no clear error messaging, leaving users unaware their submission had not registered.
  • Mandatory use while broken: The federal government maintained mandatory use requirements and enforced fines even as the app malfunctioned, placing the compliance burden on travellers rather than the defective tool.
  • No accessible alternative: Travellers without smartphones or with disabilities faced disproportionate enforcement hardship. Accommodation processes were inconsistent across border points of entry.

The approximately 10,000 wrongful quarantine orders represent a concrete harm to Canadians traceable directly to the failure to adequately test and quality-assure a product before deploying it with legal enforcement teeth. No official has faced consequences for this decision. No compensation mechanism was established for affected travellers. The Auditor General's report notes that CBSA lacked adequate processes to track, document, and remediate these cases.

06Auditor General Findings — 2023 Special Report

In February 2023, Auditor General Karen Hogan tabled a Special Examination report on ArriveCAN before Parliament. The report was the product of a targeted audit of CBSA's pandemic contracting for the application. Its findings were unambiguous: systemic procurement failures, missing documentation, and a contracting approach that violated Treasury Board policies throughout.

“This is the most glaring example of a lack of documentation, a lack of due diligence, a lack of oversight that I have ever seen.” — Auditor General Karen Hogan, testimony before the House of Commons Standing Committee on Government Operations and Estimates (OGGO), March 2023
“We found that the Canada Border Services Agency could not demonstrate that it received value for money for the ArriveCAN application. CBSA provided insufficient documentation to support millions of dollars in contracts.” — Report of the Auditor General of Canada to the House of Commons — ArriveCAN Application, 2023, para. 5
“The contracting practices we observed for ArriveCAN were not consistent with federal procurement policies. In particular, we found that contracts were awarded without a competitive process even when doing so was not justified by emergency circumstances.” — Report of the Auditor General of Canada, 2023 — Key Finding on Procurement Process

Key AG Findings — Summary

  • 📋 Missing contracts and invoices: CBSA could not produce complete contracts, invoices, or statements of work for a material portion of the $59.5M billed. Payments were approved without adequate supporting documentation.
  • 📋 No competitive process — unjustified: The emergency exception to competitive bidding was invoked well beyond its legitimate scope. Most of the contracting period was not a genuine emergency precluding competition.
  • 📋 Value for money undemonstrable: CBSA was unable to demonstrate that the public received fair value for the $59.5M expended. Cost benchmarks, deliverable records, and performance metrics were absent or inadequate.
  • 📋 CBSA management failures: Senior management oversight of the contract was inadequate. Risk management practices were not applied. The contracting environment allowed significant irregularities to persist without correction.
  • 📋 App could have cost far less: The AG noted that the application was not technically complex and that international comparisons suggested far lower costs were achievable for equivalent functionality.
  • 📋 Treasury Board policy violations: Multiple Treasury Board Secretariat procurement policies were violated throughout the contracting lifecycle, including policies on documentation, competition, and oversight.

07Parliamentary Accountability Failures

In a functional democracy, parliamentary committees serve as the primary accountability mechanism for executive branch conduct between elections. In the ArriveCAN scandal, the system was tested — and found to fall short. Witnesses refused to answer questions under oath. Ministers claimed ignorance. The committee was forced to pursue contempt proceedings against witnesses who stonewalled. Meanwhile, the officials most directly responsible remained employed within the public service.

Kristian Firth & Darren Anthony — GCStrategies
Primary contractors — summoned to OGGO committee multiple times
Repeatedly declined to identify the subcontractors who received public money. Invoked privacy concerns and potential RCMP investigation interference as justifications for non-disclosure. Initially declined to appear; when they did appear, answers were characterized by MPs across party lines as evasive and insufficient. Both were subsequently found in contempt of Parliament for failing to comply with committee orders to produce documents.

08RCMP Investigation Status

In March 2023, the Royal Canadian Mounted Police confirmed it had opened a criminal investigation into the ArriveCAN contracting process. This followed referrals from the Auditor General's findings and parliamentary committee pressure. As of April 2025, the investigation remains open with no publicly announced charges.

09Criminal Code Analysis

The following is an analysis of Criminal Code of Canada provisions potentially applicable to the ArriveCAN contracting conduct as documented by the Auditor General and parliamentary committees. This is analytical opinion based on documented facts — not legal advice, and not an assertion that any specific individual will be or should be charged. The RCMP investigation will determine whether charges are warranted.

Section Offence Potentially Applicable Conduct Applies?
s. 380(1) Fraud Over $5,000 Billing for undocumented or non-existent deliverables; obtaining public money by false pretences through inflated invoices or phantom work. POTENTIALLY YES
s. 121(1)(a) Fraud on the Government Obtaining a benefit from the government through corruption or collusion; contractor conduct in obtaining contracts without fair competition through improper relationships with public servants. POTENTIALLY YES
s. 122 Breach of Trust by Public Officer Public servants who approved payments without documentation, waived procurement requirements without authority, or approved contracts in violation of known policies. Requires proof of wilful breach. POTENTIALLY YES
s. 139(2) Obstruct Justice Refusal to comply with parliamentary committee orders to produce documents; concealment of subcontractor identities potentially relevant to a criminal investigation; contempt findings as predicate conduct. UNDER REVIEW
s. 380(2) Fraud Affecting the Public Market Where false or misleading representations were made to affect the public procurement market — for example, misrepresenting company capabilities to secure contracts. POSSIBLY
s. 426 Secret Commissions Payment or acceptance of commissions from parties with an interest in a contract, without disclosure to the principal — potentially relevant to multi-layer fee arrangements undisclosed to CBSA. POSSIBLY
⚖ Legal Analysis Note

Proving criminal fraud on the government (s.121) requires establishing: (1) a benefit was obtained, (2) by or through a public servant, (3) that was corruptly given or received. The AG's documentation of systematic procurement policy violations — while serious — does not automatically constitute criminal conduct. The RCMP must establish criminal intent (mens rea) beyond the institutional negligence the AG documented.

Breach of trust (s.122) is highly fact-specific. Courts have found that simple administrative incompetence does not meet the threshold; wilful and deliberate abuse of a position of trust is required. The line between gross negligence and criminal breach of trust is exactly what the RCMP investigation must establish.

10Call to Action — Demand Accountability

The ArriveCAN scandal is not a closed chapter. The RCMP investigation is ongoing. Parliamentary accountability remains incomplete. No minister has resigned. The public servants who approved payments for undocumented work remain unnamed in official records. Accountability requires continued public pressure, transparent journalism, and citizens who refuse to accept institutional silence as an answer.
