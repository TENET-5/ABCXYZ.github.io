SNC-Lavalin Affair — Obstruction of Justice & Attorney General Resignation
OBSTRUCTION Federal Liberal 2019 ETHICS 75% [keyword_override]

Attorney General Jody Wilson-Raybould resigned from Cabinet February 12, 2019, after sustained pressure from the PMO and PCO to grant engineering firm SNC-Lavalin a Deferred Prosecution Agreement (DPA) — which would have allowed the company to avoid criminal trial on fraud and bribery charges related to Libya. Wilson-Raybould's testimony before the Justice Committee (February 27, 2019) described 11 instances of "inappropriate" and "consistent" pressure from senior government officials including the PMO Chief of Staff Katie Telford, Principal Secretary Gerald Butts, Privy Council Clerk Michael Wernick, and Finance Minister Bill Morneau's Chief of Staff.

Gerald Butts resigned February 18, 2019. Michael Wernick resigned as Clerk of the Privy Council March 18, 2019. Trudeau stated publicly he was "surprised and disappointed" by Wilson-Raybould's resignation. The RCMP subsequently opened a criminal investigation; as of 2024 no charges have been laid against individuals in the PMO. SNC-Lavalin's engineering arm was eventually sold; the parent company rebranded as AtkinsRéalis.

Parliamentary Finding Wilson-Raybould testimony: 11 separate instances of inappropriate pressure to interfere with the Director of Public Prosecutions' independent decision. House Justice Committee majority (Liberal) voted to end hearings before all witnesses could testify. Committee chair Anthony Housefather (Liberal) broke from party line, stating the hearings should continue.
Key Individuals Justin Trudeau (PM) · Katie Telford (PMO CoS) · Gerald Butts (Principal Secretary, resigned) · Michael Wernick (Privy Council Clerk, resigned) · Bill Morneau (Finance Minister) · Jody Wilson-Raybould (AG, resigned) · Jane Philpott (Treasury Board, resigned in solidarity)

Sources: Wilson-Raybould testimony, House Justice Committee, February 27, 2019 (Hansard); Butts resignation statement February 18, 2019; Wernick resignation letter March 18, 2019; CBC/Globe and Mail reporting February–March 2019; RCMP investigation announcement 2019.

WE Charity Scandal — $912M Student Grant Contract & Conflict of Interest
CONFLICT OF INTEREST Federal Liberal 2020 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

In June 2020, the Trudeau government announced WE Charity would administer a $912M Canada Student Service Grant (CSSG) program without a competitive procurement process. The sole-source decision was later revealed to have been made despite the Trudeau family's extensive paid speaking history with WE: Margaret Trudeau had received $312,000 in fees from WE events; Alexandre Trudeau had received $32,000; Finance Minister Bill Morneau's family had received $41,366 in travel expenses paid by WE for a 2017 trip to Kenya (Morneau later reimbursed this and donated $100,000 to WE).

The Ethics Commissioner launched investigations into both Trudeau and Morneau. Morneau resigned as Finance Minister August 17, 2020 — the same day he admitted he had not paid WE back for the 2017 travel until after the scandal broke. Trudeau prorogued Parliament August 18, 2020, ending four House committee investigations. The Ethics Commissioner's investigation into Trudeau (his third ethics investigation) was halted by prorogation. WE Charity subsequently wound down Canadian operations.

Ethics Commissioner Finding Ethics Commissioner Mario Dion had previously found Trudeau violated Section 9 of the Conflict of Interest Act in the SNC-Lavalin affair (2019). The WE investigation was halted by prorogation before conclusion. Morneau investigation: Commissioner found Morneau had breached the Act by failing to recuse himself given undisclosed travel benefit.
Parliamentary Response Parliament prorogued August 18, 2020 — one day after Morneau's resignation — ending Finance, Ethics, Access to Information, and Status of Women committee investigations simultaneously. Opposition parties characterized prorogation as deliberate obstruction of accountability.

Sources: House Finance Committee testimony June–August 2020; Ethics Commissioner Mario Dion WE investigation; Globe and Mail reporting June–August 2020; Morneau resignation statement August 17, 2020; WE Charity financial disclosures tabled to committee.

Senate Expenses Scandal — Duffy, Wallin, Brazeau & PMO Cover-Up
FRAUD Federal Conservative 2013–2016 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Senators Mike Duffy, Pamela Wallin, and Patrick Brazeau were suspended without pay in November 2013 for improper expense claims. Duffy claimed the primary residence allowance for his PEI cottage while living in Ottawa; total irregular claims: $90,172. PMO Chief of Staff Nigel Wright secretly arranged a $90,172 personal cheque to repay Duffy's expenses — initially described as Wright acting alone; later RCMP investigation revealed extensive PMO involvement in coordinating the cover-up. Wright was charged with bribery; charges were later stayed. Duffy was charged with 31 counts of fraud, bribery and breach of trust; acquitted on all counts in 2016 (Justice Vaillancourt found Duffy was given conflicting advice by Senate administration itself).

RCMP Investigation Finding RCMP Corporal Greg Horton's ITO (Information to Obtain) documents (2013) revealed 13 PMO staff were aware of the Wright-Duffy arrangement. Emails showed PMO staff worked to craft a Senate report finding that would exonerate Duffy. PM Harper's claim that Wright acted "on his own initiative" contradicted by RCMP documents showing coordinated PMO messaging strategy.

Sources: RCMP ITO documents (released 2013–2014); R. v. Duffy, Ontario Superior Court of Justice, Justice Charles Vaillancourt, April 21, 2016; Auditor General Senate expense audit June 2015; Senate Hansard suspension debate November 5, 2013.

Sponsorship Scandal (Adscam) — $100M in Fraudulent Contracts, Gomery Commission
FRAUD Federal Liberal 1996–2004 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

The federal Sponsorship Program, created after the 1995 Quebec referendum to promote Canadian unity, became the vehicle for a systematic scheme to funnel public money to Liberal-friendly Quebec advertising firms. Auditor General Sheila Fraser's February 2004 report found $100M of the $250M program had been paid to advertising agencies for little or no work. The Gomery Commission (2004–2006), led by Justice John Gomery, found that public funds were "used for the benefit of the Liberal Party" and that senior government officials had participated in organizing kickbacks from advertising agencies back to the Liberal Party of Canada.

Prime Minister Jean Chrétien was found by Gomery to bear "indirect" responsibility; PM Paul Martin was exonerated. Several Liberal operatives were convicted: Jacques Corriveau received a suspended sentence; Jean Brault received 30 months; Charles Guíté received 42 months. The scandal contributed directly to the Liberal defeat in January 2006.

Gomery Commission Finding (2006) "A complex web of financial transactions among the Government of Canada, the Liberal Party of Canada and a number of Quebec advertising and communications companies resulted in property of the Government of Canada being diverted for the benefit of the Liberal Party of Canada." — Phase 2 Report, p. 16.

Sources: Auditor General Sheila Fraser, February 2004 Report; Gomery Commission Phase 1 Report "Who is Responsible?" November 2005; Gomery Commission Phase 2 Report "Restoring Accountability" February 2006; R. v. Brault; R. v. Guíté; RCMP investigation records.

Foreign Interference — NSICOP: China & India Targeted Canadian Democracy
NATIONAL SECURITY Federal Liberal 2019–2024 ETHICS 80% [NPU]

The National Security and Intelligence Committee of Parliamentarians (NSICOP) June 2024 Special Report found that China, India, Pakistan and other states conducted systematic foreign interference operations targeting Canadian federal elections in 2019 and 2021. The report found that "some Members of Parliament are, wittingly or not, advancing the efforts of foreign states." CSIS had briefed the PMO and PCO repeatedly on interference activities; the government did not act to remove or investigate potentially compromised MPs.

The Public Inquiry into Foreign Interference (Hogue Commission) interim report (May 2024) found interference "probably" affected outcomes in at least one 2021 riding. RCMP opened criminal investigations; as of 2024 no Members of Parliament had been charged. The government's handling of CSIS warnings — particularly the 2021 briefings about Beijing-linked networks — became a central accountability question.

NSICOP Special Report Finding (June 2024) "Some of the foreign interference activities directed at recent federal general elections were successful." The report further found the government "failed to adequately respond to the threat" despite repeated CSIS warnings. The report was partially redacted before public release; opposition parties unanimously demanded full declassification.

Sources: NSICOP Special Report on Foreign Interference in Canadian Federal Elections and Democratic Institutions, June 2024; Public Inquiry into Foreign Interference (Hogue Commission) Interim Report, May 3, 2024; CSIS Annual Public Reports 2021–2023; House National Security Committee hearings 2023.

Bill C-7 — MAID Expansion: Safeguards Removed, Mental Illness Expansion
LEGISLATION Federal Liberal 2021–2024 ETHICS 99% [NPU]

Bill C-7 (Royal Assent March 17, 2021) removed the 10-day reflection period, eliminated the requirement for two independent witnesses, and created a new Track 2 for non-terminal illness. The 90-day assessment period for Track 2 was criticized by disability rights advocates as insufficient. Senate Amendment 9 scheduled MAID eligibility for mental illness as sole underlying condition (MAID-MD-SUMC) for March 17, 2023; this was subsequently delayed three times (to March 2024, then March 2027).

Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister of Justice Gary Anandasangaree stated in committee that the government had consulted "extensively" with disability groups; the Council of Canadians with Disabilities and Inclusion Canada disputed this, stating they were not meaningfully consulted. Health Canada's own MAID Annual Reports show deaths under Track 2 rising sharply: 463 in 2021, 1,242 in 2022, 1,823 in 2023.

Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs (2021) Senators Brent Cotter and Kim Pate filed dissenting opinions. Senator Pate's dissent: "We are deeply concerned that C-7, as amended, will result in the deaths of people with disabilities and mental illness who are not dying but who are suffering as a result of social and economic conditions that the state has failed to remedy."

Sources: Bill C-7 (SC 2021 c.2); Health Canada MAID Annual Reports 2021–2023; Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs, March 2021; Council of Canadians with Disabilities submissions; Hansard Second Reading debate February–March 2021.
